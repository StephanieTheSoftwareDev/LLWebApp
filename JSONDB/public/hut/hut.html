Make Sure It's a Strong Mark



Distinctive marks are strong, and strong marks gain the most protection. Marks that are considered “fanciful” are considered the strongest marks, and they are given the greatest protection. Generic marks won’t get trademark protection.





Fanciful Marks Arbitrary Marks Suggestive Marks Descriptive Marks Generic Marks
Fanciful Marks

A fanciful mark gains the most protection because it was invented solely to serve as a trademark and has no other meaning. Examples are “Google” and “Uber.”

These words are typically unknown or completely outside of common usage.
Arbitrary Marks

Arbitrary marks are also strong. These words are common but are used in an uncommon way, and do not describe or suggest the product or service.

An example is “Apple” to describe computers.
Suggestive Marks

Suggestive marks require people to imagine what the nature of a product or service might be.
For example, “Microsoft” suggests software, but it is not a literal description of it.
Descriptive Marks

Descriptive marks are those that clearly describe a product or service, and no additional thought or imagination is required to determine what the product or service is.

Descriptive marks are weak and do not qualify for trademark protection, unless a secondary meaning has developed over time. An example is “Smith’s Plumbing.”
Generic Marks

Generic marks are terms that cannot be used as trademarks. For example, “email” would be a generic mark.

Trademarks can become generic if the public misuses the mark to generally identify goods or services.

Make Sure You Have the Right to Use



Before selecting a mark, you should determine whether you can register it with the United States Patent and Trademark Office. Business owners invest time and money building the reputation associated with the trademark. For this reason, failure to do a comprehensive trademark search at the beginning of operations is unwise. Someone else might already have rights in a trademark that is similar to a name you are considering for your product or service, and if you fail to do a search, you take a risk that you will receive a notice from such trademark owner that demands that you stop using the trademark and start over. Further, you could be served with a lawsuit and need to hire an attorney, which can be very time consuming and costly. By conducting a trademark search ahead of time, you protect your company's investment in building the brand.




Conduct a Trademark Search

Search federal, state, and online databases to make sure someone else does not already have rights to use your proposed mark. A federal search can be done for free on the USPTO website using the Trademark Electronic Search System (TESS) here: tmsearch.USPTO.gov When a trademark is federally registered with the USPTO, a court will assume the defendant knew it was federally registered. A defendant may be required to pay damages and the trademark owner's attorney's fees. In many states, the trademark database is part of the Secretary of State's office, but in some states, it is a separate department. In CA, info can be found here: sos.ca.gov It is also a good idea to search for unregistered trademarks via a basic online Google search to see if anyone is using the mark for a similar purpose. You may conduct a basic domain search here: WHOIS.ICANN.org




Use in Commerce Reqs

Basic rights in the trademark are created simply by using the trademark in business. This use must be in alignment with trademark law, such as exercising "good faith." The USPTO also allows trademark filings for an ‘intent-to-use’ application, when the company is planning on using the mark in the future. This can be helpful for a startup or entrepreneur.

Determine What Type of Mark You Have





Trademark Service Mark Certification Mark Collective Mark
Trademark

Trademarks are words, phrases, symbols, designs, or a combination that can be used to identify and distinguish the source of goods. A trademark is often the most important intangible asset that a company owns.
Service Mark

Service marks are words, phrases, symbols, designs, or a combination that are used to identify and distinguish the source of services.
Certification Mark

Certification marks certify that goods or services have certain characteristics, origin, or that certain standards are upheld in manufacturing.

An example of a certification mark is the Good Housekeeping Seal of Approval.
Collective Marks

Organizations sometimes use words, phrases, symbols, designs, or a combination that show that the person displaying the mark is a member. The organization is the mark’s owner.

In order to belong to a group and use the collective mark, a member usually pays a membership fee and follows rules. Collective membership marks can help the public know whether a business is affiliated with a group.

Your Common Law Rights Without Registering





Ownership of a mark gives the owner the right to exclude others from using similar marks if those marks may cause confusion. The laws surrounding unregistered marks are derived from principles of unfair competition or unfair business. Specifically, it is considered unfair for somebody to trade on the goodwill you have built up around a mark in order to sell competing goods or services.


Using a trademark in commerce gives you rights under “common law." This means that you may be able to stop others from using your mark if they started using it after you, and if they are selling similar products or services. Unregistered marks are protected within a particular geographic area and reasonable extensions only.


You can claim trademark rights in your unregistered trademark as long as it is distinctive and identifies or distinguishes your products or services. A trade name for your business is not the same as an unregistered trademark and is not given the same protections under federal trademark law.

Benefits of Registering a Trademark





A trademark is a form of intellectual property, an asset that adds value to a business. Registration can become valuable after a business grows, even if the value is not immediately apparent.


There are numerous advantages to registering a trademark including nationwide protection, ability to file a lawsuit against an infringer in federal court, incontestable status after five years of registration, presumption of ownership, enhanced remedies, and constructive notice of ownership to others around the nation. Incontestability can be helpful because a trademark cannot be attacked for being merely “descriptive,” even if it is.


Registration may also help when fighting for a domain name. The trademark owner can use the registration as a basis for registration abroad, and file the registration with the U.S. Customs Service to stop others from importing infringing foreign goods.

Official Registration



Prior to filing, gather the following information:

  • Name(s) of the applicant(s)
  • Address of the applicant
  • Citizenship of the applicant(s), or, if the applicant is a corporation, association, partnership, or other entity, the jurisdiction under the laws of which the applicant is organized
  • If the applicant is a domestic partnership, the names and citizenship of the general partners
  • If the applicant is a domestic joint venture, the names and citizenship of the active members of the joint venture
  • One or more bases for filing the application. Have you used it in commerce or do you have intent-to-use?
  • A list of the particular goods or services in connection with which the applicant intends to use the mark
  • The international class of goods or services, if known. See: bitlaw.com
  • If the mark is not in standard characters, a description of the mark
  • If the mark includes non-English wording, an English translation of that wording
  • A drawing. See: USPTO.gov
  • The fee. View schedule here: law.Cornell.edu



Where to File:

Visit the USPTO website to learn more and take steps to file. See: USPTO.gov




Process:

After submission, an examiner will review your application. If the examiner denies your application, you will have six months to reply or amend it. The application will then be reexamined.

This process may be repeated until the examiner issues a final refusal or you do not reply, amend, or appeal. If an examiner approves the application, it will be published in the Official Gazette of the USPTO.

This gives others the opportunity to oppose the mark. Others may oppose it if they are using a similar mark and believe your mark will dilute theirs. The person or entity opposing registration will need to show likelihood of damage if your mark is registered.

A notice of registration will be published in the Official Gazette. Others have five years after your trademark is registered to file a petition to have such registration canceled.




After Filing:

As soon as you claim your right in a particular mark, you can use the ™ (trademark) or ℠ (service mark) symbol. However, you can only use the federal registration symbol of ® if the USPTO registers the mark, and the symbol with the mark can then only be used in connection with the goods or services that the USPTO lists in the trademark registration.
